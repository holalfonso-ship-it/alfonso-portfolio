01

Everyone has a vision for their home.

A color they've been meaning to try, a style they've saved a hundred times on Pinterest, a room they know could feel completely different. The vision is there. The problem is the gap between that vision and reality, the inability to see how a space will actually look before spending thousands on furniture, paint, and renovation.


That gap has a name. We called it Renovation Paralysis.


Our research confirmed what we suspected: 73% of users wanted to redecorate, but felt blocked. Not by lack of inspiration, they had plenty of that. Blocked by the fear of making an expensive mistake. So they stayed stuck, scrolling, saving, never deciding.

The insight that changed everything
"This wasn't a decoration tool.
It was a confidence tool."

When I joined this project as Lead Product Designer, the brief was to build a decoration tool. But the data told a different story. This wasn't a decoration problem, it was a confidence problem. And that reframe changed every design decision that followed.

The process, before a single screen was designed.

Before opening Figma, we ran a structured series of collaborative workshops with all stakeholders. The goal was to align everyone on what mattered, and surface the north star collectively, not impose it top-down.

WS 01

Co-creation workshops

Joint sessions with PM, PO and business stakeholders to align vision, identify opportunities and prioritise from day zero. Everyone had a voice in defining the product before a single screen existed, avoiding costly direction changes in later phases.

Facilitated
WS 02

User persona workshops

Collaborative construction of user personas with the full team. We defined who the real user was, not the imagined one, their motivations, blockers and behaviours around home decoration. These profiles directly informed design decisions, from onboarding tone to the selection of the 12 styles in the Style Library.

Facilitated
WS 03

MoSCoW prioritisation

With all stakeholders (PM, PO, Dev Lead, business), we ran a MoSCoW workshop to define what entered the MVP and what didn't. This was the framework that resolved the tension between the dev team (ship fast) and PM/PO (more features), with an objective, shared framework rather than opinion. What didn't make the Must Have or Should Have was documented and moved to the Q1 roadmap.

WS 04

North star metric workshop

In the pre-MVP workshops, the team collectively identified the need for a single guiding metric. The result was time to first redesign: the time from app open to first AI-generated room transformation. It wasn't imposed, it emerged from the process. That guaranteed PM, devs and design evaluated every decision against the same criterion.

From the workshops, we moved into quantitative research. A survey of 340 users confirmed and sharpened the problem. 73% had abandoned a redecoration project due to fear of making costly mistakes. The insight didn't just validate the problem, it reframed the entire product strategy.

05

Three decisions that shaped the product.

01

Automate the masking: hide the complexity

The original flow required users to manually mask their room before the AI could process it. This was technically necessary but UX-catastrophic for a B2C audience.

Decision: moved room masking entirely into the ControlNet pipeline. The user never sees it. One tap, done. This single change removed 4 steps from the flow.
02

Wow first, controls second

The granular editing layer (wall colour picker, furniture swap by category, lighting tone) existed in v1 as part of the primary flow. It was overwhelming users before they'd experienced any value.

Decision: moved all editing controls to a secondary layer. Available, but not required. Users reach the wow moment first, then explore customisation at their own pace.
03

A roadmap instead of an argument

Competitors had more features in their MVPs. The dev team wanted to ship fast. PM/PO wanted more features before launch. As the sole designer at the centre of this tension, I needed more than an opinion.

Decision: built a Q1 post-launch roadmap showing exactly when each competitor feature would arrive. It gave stakeholders certainty, devs permission to ship, and business the visibility to plan CRO campaigns. The roadmap unlocked the agreement, not a debate.

07

What testing changed.

Validation wasn't a checkbox at the end of the process, it was what shaped the final decisions. Two findings directly changed the product.

Maze · n=200+ users
Style Library validation
Each of the 12 interior styles was tested for instant recognition and desirability. Only styles with high recognition and desirability scores made it into the product.
Usability Testing
Two things that changed
Full flow testing surfaced two clear issues.
Paint was redundant with Wall Paint. Removed to eliminate confusion.
Home cards didn't show Before/After. Users didn't understand the core value proposition. Redesigned cards and Home to make the transformation immediately visible.
A/B Testing
Pricing pages
Plans and copy were tested from launch to optimise conversion. Results fed continuous iterations on pricing pages through Q1.
Amplitude · Hotjar
Behavioural data
Session tracking and heatmaps used throughout to identify friction points in the flow and validate improvements after each release.

09

What I'd do differently.

01

The onboarding was a missed opportunity

We based the onboarding on competitive benchmarking, we replicated what competitors were doing without questioning it enough. In retrospect, there was a real opportunity for differentiation. Onboarding is the first moment a user forms their perception of the product, and ours could have reinforced the "confidence tool" positioning much more strongly instead of replicating what already existed.

02

The next frontier is planning, not just visualising

The MVP validated the visualisation concept. But the bigger opportunity is converting the app into a full renovation planning tool: AI-generated floor plans, cost estimation, material selection. That vision was visible from the start, but required the foundation the MVP built first.

Within 90 days, the app had become a daily habit. Users weren't opening it once, they were coming back 4.2 times a week, exploring different styles, different rooms, different possibilities.

The paralysis had broken. +68% purchase confidence was the proof we'd solved the right problem. Not decoration. Confidence.
