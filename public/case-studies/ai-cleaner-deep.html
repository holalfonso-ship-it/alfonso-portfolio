01

The app was good. The first 60 seconds were not.

AI Cleaner had a clear value proposition: scan your iPhone, identify duplicates, screenshots, and junk files, and recover storage in seconds. The problem was that users were never getting there. They downloaded the app, opened it, hit the permissions screen, and left.

Amplitude told us the exact number: 67% of users abandoned at the photo library permissions request, before they had seen a single feature. We had built a product that worked, but an entry experience that didn't earn the trust required to use it.

The product challenge wasn't the AI, the scan speed, or the storage math. It was a trust problem at the very first moment of the relationship.

The real problem

"The problem wasn't that users didn't want to clean their phone. It was that we were asking them to hand over access to every photo they'd ever taken before we'd given them a single reason to trust us."

That reframe changed everything. Once we stopped treating onboarding as a setup flow and started treating it as a trust-building sequence, the right decisions became obvious: show value first, ask for access second. The permissions screen wasn't the problem — the order was.

02

My role: design lead across two markets.

I joined as Lead Product Designer with ownership across the full product experience: onboarding, core scan flow, design system, and the China market variant. The team was small but fast — and the brief was clear: fix activation, then scale globally.

The north star wasn't downloads. It was the number of users who completed a scan and recovered storage on their first session. Everything we designed had to move that number.

03

Four weeks to understand two different problems.

Before touching any screens, we needed to understand why activation was failing and what the China expansion would require. We ran a structured discovery sprint: quantitative first, qualitative second, competitive last.

Week 01

Funnel analysis with Amplitude

We mapped every step of the activation funnel and identified the 67% drop-off at permissions. We also found a secondary drop-off: 31% of users who granted permissions still didn't complete a scan. Two problems, not one. The permissions screen was the first; the scan result UX was the second.

Amplitude · Cohort analysis
Week 02

Qualitative research on trust and permission anxiety

We ran 12 moderated sessions with users who had abandoned during onboarding. The pattern was consistent: users weren't opposed to granting access, they were opposed to granting it blind. They wanted to understand what the app would do with their photos before handing them over. The copy and context around the permissions ask was doing no work.

User interviews · Synthetic Users validation
Week 03

China market research: different trust patterns, different density expectations

For the China variant, we ran a benchmarking sprint against the top 10 utility apps on the Chinese App Store. Key differences: higher information density is expected, not overwhelming; WeChat and Alipay are trust anchors, not distractions; social proof (ratings, user counts) needs to appear higher in the flow. The mental model for "a trustworthy app" is structurally different.

Competitive audit · Market benchmarking
Week 04

Synthesis: one product, two activation problems, one design system

The discovery output was a single strategic recommendation: redesign the onboarding trust layer for the US market, and build a China-adapted variant from the same component system rather than a parallel product. Separate products meant separate debt. One token layer, two configurations, meant a team of three could maintain both without doubling the workload.

Three decisions that changed the trajectory.

01

Defer the permissions ask until after demonstrated value

Instead of asking for photo library access on screen 2, we built a demo scan mode that showed users what the app would find — using sample data — before asking for anything. The permissions request moved to the moment of intent: after the user had seen the value and tapped "scan my photos."

Why this over a better permissions screen: A better-designed permissions screen would still be asking for trust before earning it. The demo scan addressed the root cause, not the symptom. Maze testing with 80 users confirmed the hypothesis: demo-first increased permissions grant rate by 2.1x over a redesigned-screen-only approach.
02

Reduce the scan flow from 7 steps to 2

The original flow had seven distinct screens between opening the app and completing a clean. We collapsed this to two: a scan progress screen and a results screen with a single primary action. Every intermediate screen that asked the user to make a decision was either eliminated or deferred to an advanced settings section.

Why this over a progressive disclosure approach: Progressive disclosure still requires decisions at each step. Our research showed that users who abandoned post-permissions were abandoning because the cleanup felt complicated, not because they lacked information. Simplicity was the goal, not depth. The 31% secondary drop-off fell to under 8% after the redesign.
03

Build one design system for two markets, not two products

The China variant required higher information density, different social proof patterns, WeChat and Alipay payment entry points, and a distinct visual trust hierarchy. The temptation was to build it as a separate product. Instead, we built it as a configuration of the same component system — a single token layer with market-specific overrides for density, typography scale, and payment UI.

Why this mattered strategically: Two parallel products would have doubled maintenance costs and halved the team's ability to ship features consistently. The one-system approach meant a bug fixed in the US variant was fixed in China simultaneously, and a new component built for the US was already 80% of the way to the China variant. This DesignOps decision was as much a business decision as a design one.

06

We tested before we built. Every time.

With a team of three and engineering bandwidth always at a premium, we couldn't afford to build features that failed in testing. Every major decision went through at least one validation round before a single line of code was written.

Maze · Unmoderated testing
Permissions flow A/B
80 participants tested the demo-first onboarding against a redesigned traditional permissions screen. Two variations, same sample cohort, measured on permissions grant rate and time-to-first-scan.
Outcome: Demo-first variant achieved 2.1x higher permissions grant rate. Decision confirmed before any engineering work began.
Amplitude · Post-launch
Funnel tracking at every step
After launch, we instrumented every screen transition in the new flow. The 31% secondary drop-off (post-permissions, pre-scan-completion) was tracked weekly and drove two additional iterations on the results screen CTA hierarchy.
Outcome: Secondary drop-off reduced from 31% to under 8% over 6 weeks post-launch.
Synthetic Users · Market validation
China variant concept testing
Before investing engineering time in the China-specific payment integrations and density variants, we ran concept validation with Synthetic Users — AI-simulated Chinese market personas — to pressure-test the information hierarchy and trust signal placement.
Outcome: Identified two trust signal placements that were counterintuitive for Chinese users, corrected before prototype build.
App Store · Qualitative reviews
Post-launch sentiment analysis
We tracked App Store review sentiment weekly in both markets post-launch, using a structured tagging system to identify recurring UX friction patterns. This became an ongoing input into the sprint planning process, not a one-off post-launch check.
Outcome: Review sentiment identified the storage recovered metric as the most motivating element — surfaced it more prominently in v1.1.

08

What I'd do differently.

01

I'd instrument the demo scan earlier

The demo scan was our most effective onboarding intervention, but we didn't have robust analytics on it until two months after launch. We knew it was working from the permissions grant rate, but we didn't understand which elements of the demo were driving conviction — the file type categories, the storage number, or the animation. Earlier instrumentation would have let us optimise the demo faster and with more confidence.

02

The China variant deserved its own discovery sprint

We validated the China concepts with Synthetic Users, which was faster than in-person research but came with a ceiling on fidelity. The two trust signal corrections we made pre-launch were caught by that process, but I suspect there were subtler issues we missed — particularly around the post-scan flow and the way results are communicated. A dedicated round of research with real Chinese users would have given us the confidence to ship the China variant with fewer post-launch question marks.

03

DesignOps should have been framed as a business case from day one

The decision to build one design system for two markets instead of two parallel products saved us significant time and cost. But I presented it as a design architecture decision, not a business case. In retrospect, I should have framed the ROI explicitly from the start: one system means half the maintenance debt, half the risk of divergence, and a team that can ship consistently into both markets without burning out. That framing would have got buy-in faster and with less friction from the PM and engineering leads.

The 67% drop-off wasn't a design problem. It was a strategy problem — we were asking for trust before we'd earned it. Once we named that clearly, the right decisions followed.

Building for two markets from one system wasn't the easy path. But it was the right one — and it's the kind of decision that only looks obvious in retrospect.
